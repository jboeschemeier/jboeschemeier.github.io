The Impact of Technological Progress on Labour Markets: Policy Challenges

  • December 12, 2019
  • By Armands Timsans

The article provides an overview of the current thinking about the impact of technological progress on labour markets and follows up with the policy challenges associated with the Digital Revolution. Goos does so by taking a closer look at skill-biased technological change (SBTC), capital-skill complementarity (CSC), routine-biased technological change (RBTC), as well as, briefly, takes a look at other, alternative explanations. He then explores various policies related to education and training, labour market, income redistribution, and technology regulation.

The Impact of Technological Progress on Labour Markets

SBTC

Generally, the SBTC hypothesis can be seen as a way to think about the interaction between technological change and differently skilled labour. Most importantly SBTC is based on the assumption that technological progress enhances skilled labour productivity more than it does that of unskilled labour. Moreover, the framework predicts an increase in the relative wages for skilled labour in case of increased demand for skilled labour and a decrease in the relative wages in case of increased relative supply of skilled labour.

For more on SBTC, refer to:
Acemoglu, D. (2002): “Technical Change, Inequality and the Labor Market”, Journal of Economic Literature, 40(1), pp. 7-72.

CSC

CSC, unlike SBTC, assumes that technological progress does not increase labour productivity of skilled labour more than it does for unskilled labour. The CSC hypothesis considers capital as a third input factor in production, assuming that technological progress is best captured by decreasing prices of capital over time. The hypothesis revolves around the idea that substitutability between capital and skilled labour is lower than that of capital and unskilled labour, meaning that capital and skill are relative complements.

RBTC

Unlike the previous two hypotheses RBTC assumes that the Digital Revolution directly replaces workers that are doing routine, codifiable tasks, resulting in job polarisation of employment, rather than skill-upgrading as is the case for the previous two hypotheses. Additionally, RBTC assumes worker self-selection of different skill levels across different tasks according to comparative advantage. The model developed by Acemoglu and Autor (2011) assumes that task inputs are imperfect substitutes in aggregate output, and each task is produced using labour from each of three skill groups, namely, low, medium, and high, as well as capital, where each type of labour and capital are perfect substitutes but differ in their task efficiencies. This in turn, led them to the hypothesis that digital capital has a comparative advantage in doing routine, easily codifiable tasks in a manner that displaces medium-skilled labour from their jobs, leading to polarisation.

For more on RBTC, refer to:
Goos, M., A. Manning and A. Salomons (2014): “Explaining Job Polarization: Routine-Biased Technological Change and Offshoring”, The American Economic Review, 104(8), pp. 2509-2526.

Equilibrium Impacts of Technological Progress

Autor (2015) mentions two equilibrium channels that can either mitigate or augment the impact of technological progress on employment and wages. The first being technology-induced changes in labour supply, where polarisation and wage polarisation direction depends on the labour supply elasticity of middle-skilled labour relative to that of low and high skilled workers. Second factor mentioned by Autor are the technology-induced changes in relative output prices and real income depending on the price and income elasticities of consumer demand. Effectively meaning that between-sector job polarisation can be explained by technology-induced changes in relative product demand.

Alternative Explanations

The possible explanations for the post 2000 period. characterised by a relatively weak employment growth of high-paying sectors, include the boom-and-bust cycle, offshoring and the increased competition from China.

Policy Challenges for Labour Markets from Digital Progress

Education and Training Policies

One policy idea is that investing in high-tech education could solve the issues of high-skilled labour, effectively dampening the skill premium, however such implementations are highly reliant on governments, that have, so far, failed to increase expenditures on such ideas. Other ideas focus fields such as science, technology, engineering, maths fields, medical professions, and teaching, fields that are less susceptible to automation, require high flexibility and non-routing cognitive processing. Additionally, future policies should take into account the less-educated workers as well, where investments in non-routine skills still require large amounts of attention.

Labour Market Policies

Such policies seek to modernise the processes of public employments services, as well as various offline and online job-matching platforms to increase the matching probabilities of both, job-seekers and businesses.

Income Redistribution Policies

As an outcome of job polarisation, the winners and losers of the labour market will face increased inequality, including, inter-generational income mobility restrictions. One of the biggest controversies in terms of labour and inequality is the fact that labour income tax is higher than capital income tax, even though both compete for the same jobs. Giving way to one of the most prolific policies - changes in the taxation system, such that the benefits of the Digital Revolution are shared broadly.

Technology Regulation Policies

Because the success of technologies is measured in terms of profit or efficiency, such policies would look at the implementation and design of technologies in a manner that would also consider the potential consequences for workers.

Conclusions

Technological advances, especially, RBTC, that leads to an excess supply of medium-skilled workers, results in an economy-wide job polarisation, where middle-skilled workers have to find jobs either moving up the task complexity ladder or down, are transforming the labour markets. To counter such changes, the main measures involve policies, that would either protect the labour, redirect the labour towards a more complementary future with technology via education and training, enhance worker-employer matching possibilities or income redistribution policies.
