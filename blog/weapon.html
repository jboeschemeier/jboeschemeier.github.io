The Future of Warfare - An A.I. Issue

  • December 13, 2019
  • By Jonas Boeschemeier

What if facial recognition does not just allow you to unlock your phone by looking at it, but can also be used to track you, identify you and to kill you? Unfortunately, this is not a farfetched dystopian future but actually closer to reality than most would believe. The applications of A.I. for warfare are quite extensive, and some might argue for the better of humanity, while others are strongly opposed to it.

The video you see above was posted today on the New York Times YouTube Channel and deals with the topic of how A.I. and new technologies could be used to revolutionise the way of how wars are fought and if there is a need to regulate research in that direction. It addresses the opportunities and risks of sending machines on the battlefield instead of humans. Machines do not get tired, they do not do mistakes and they process information faster and more efficiently. But at the same time, machines are not taught moral or ethics. They follow the objective they were programmed to follow and cannot distinguish beyond that – cannot distinguish between good and bad. For that reason, there is a global wide movement to regulate the research conducted that aims to develop lethal autonomous machines, based on the underlying belief that no autonomous robot should decide about whether or not to take another human’s life. Advocates of the development of such technologies argue that they could potentially save lives. Machines will fight the war of the future instead of humans, and that even more efficient. One machine on the battlefield can replace dozens of soldiers and have the same lethality. That however seems to be a recurrent theme. The Gatling Gun was also intended to reduce the casualties of war by allowing four soldiers to have the same firepower as 100. However, as time went on wars were not coined by a reduction in casualties, but by an even greater magnitude of destruction that was unimaginable before. It is a highly debated and complicated topic, where diplomacy, technology and ethics clash on each other, but for now I would like to end this post with the same quote on which the video ended:

No country is ever going to surrender until the costs that they have incurred are unbearable. So even if your robots are defeated, the next stage is that their robots will start killing your people, because the unfortunate reality is that wars will only end when people die.”
