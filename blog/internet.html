The Impact of the Internet on the Labour Market

  • December 08, 2019
  • By Jonas Boeschemeier

When I was around 15 years old, we had to write a paper instead of taking an exam at my school; I chose to do it in Social Sciences. I recently found this paper on my old hard-drive again and the topic of it just fits too perfectly to not share it on this website, because 15-year old me wrote about: “The Impact of the Internet on the Labour Market”. In this post, I would like to share the insights of this paper but bear in mind that the original research was done by a young teenager with no knowledge about economics or whatsoever (not like I am an expert now or anything).

I began my paper by analysing the factors of the internet that affect our everyday life, and then continued what that implies for certain services and jobs. Essentially, we become more and more independent from our location through the internet. This can be seen through three channels:

With the internet, we can more easily communicate with our friends, colleagues, etc., via services like e-mail or messengers. Thus, communication was heavily influenced, and some may argue, improved by the internet, which caused former jobs that enabled communication between parties to become more obsolete.

Secondly, the way how we obtain information has drastically changed with the introduction of the internet. The internet provides us with an enormous library filled with all kind of information and data available from our home desktop. Therefore, jobs that inform us about sectors that were previously untransparent and not accessible to the public might be substituted by the internet.

Lastly, the internet allows us to conduct interactions, e.g. a majority of people shop online or do online-banking nowadays. The idea behind this factor is that previously difficult types of interactions can now be completed comfortably from home.

Impact on Labour (Market)

My analysis on how the internet impacts the labour market is four folded. First, I looked at whole sectors and how they were affected through the three aforementioned channels (communication, information, and interaction). Second, I addressed how individual jobs and their working methods might have changed due to the internet. Lastly, I examined new ways of working which arose through the internet.

Regarding communication, I compared data of the usage of e-mail and letter communication. Basically, letters are only rarely used anymore to communicate due to faster alternatives. But the affect on labour seems to miniature here. Alternatively, big social media created a lot of new jobs throughout the last decade either directly or indirectly through new advertisement opportunities. In the information case, I looked at the sector of travel agencies, which used to be the only gateway to inform yourself about travel opportunities and to book those. With the raise of the internet, many webpages emerged that allow one to read reviews, compare destination and finally to book the trip. Indeed, the amount of travel agency offices has strongly decreased in recent years. We can see the same pattern for bank branches, and that many commercial banks try to invest more in their digital infrastructure. Additionally, the significance of the interaction part of the internet can be seen with the success of the online-shopping platform Amazon. Amazon is not only successful from a consumer perspective, but also created many jobs all around the world due to the logistical challenges it needs to overcome. Most of those jobs are responsible for successfully delivering the items to us or storing them in the warehouse.

To illustrate the impact on individual jobs, I used the example of a journalist. The journalist was not many years a purely print-media based jobs, where their articles would be distributed via a newspaper to the people. Nowadays however, the internet became a crucial part on how we obtain information. We do not necessarily require newspaper subscription anymore to inform ourselves about the current situation in the world. This is a crucial part of how the journalist needed to adapt. The journalist has to make use of the new arising platform, may it be for research or publication purposes.

In the last paragraph, I stated three examples that I grouped into “new ways of working”. Firstly, the internet allowed for the opportunity to work from home. The possibilities for home-office increased considerably with the introduction of the internet. Secondly, the internal organisational structure of many companies could be influenced by the trend of cloud-computing, which would enable quicker information exchange withing the company. My third example is a concept called “crowdsourcing”, which relates to outsourcing. Through intermediary website, companies can outsource small so-called “microtasks” to individuals that have the skills and willingness to complete this task for a small compensation. The well-known website Wikipedia is somewhat based on this system just without compensation. All the information on Wikipedia comes from various individuals that volunteered to write an article on that website, without this “crowd” Wikipedia would have not grown to the largest online encyclopedia.

After paraphrasing my four-year-old paper and pointing out its main conclusions, I am realising that the paper had less to do with the labour market, but rather addressed how the ways we work have changed. Nevertheless, I found it really interesting to have this small throw-back and I hope that you also learnt something new from it.
